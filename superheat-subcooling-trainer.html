Superheat & subcooling, plainly

Two numbers tell you almost everything about how a system is running and whether the charge is right. Both come from one move: take a gauge pressure, convert it to the saturation temperature off the refrigerant's chart, and compare it to an actual pipe temperature. Superheat lives on the low side; subcooling lives on the high side.

Superheat

suction line temp − saturation temp (dew) · low side

It's how many degrees the vapor has been heated above its boiling point. You read it on the suction line: clamp the line temperature, take the low-side gauge pressure, convert it to saturation temperature on the dew curve, and subtract.

Why it matters

Superheat proves the last of the liquid boiled off inside the evaporator before the vapor heads back to the compressor. Too little superheat means raw liquid is leaving the coil — it slams into the compressor (floodback / slugging) and washes out the oil. Healthy superheat is the compressor's insurance policy.

Typical at the evaporator: ~8–15 °F. Measured back at the condensing unit it reads higher (total/compressor superheat, often 20 °F+) because the suction line picks up heat on the way.

Subcooling

saturation temp (bubble) − liquid line temp · high side

It's how many degrees the liquid has been cooled below its condensing point. You read it on the liquid line: take the high-side gauge pressure, convert it to saturation temperature on the bubble curve, and subtract the measured liquid-line temperature.

Why it matters

Subcooling proves a solid column of liquid — no vapor bubbles (flash gas) — is feeding the metering device. Flash gas starves the evaporator and kills capacity. Just as useful: subcooling reflects how much refrigerant is stacked in the condenser, so it rises and falls with the charge.

Typical: ~8–12 °F on a TXV system per the data plate. It's a window for sanity-checking, not a universal target — always read the plate.

The part techs get wrong

Which number you charge to depends on the metering device

Adding or removing refrigerant moves both numbers — but only one of them is a trustworthy charging target, and which one depends on whether the metering device can modulate. Pick the wrong number and you'll chase your tail (or mis-charge the system).

TXV / EEV

Charge to subcooling

A thermostatic (or electronic) expansion valve actively modulates refrigerant flow to hold a steady evaporator superheat. So when you add or pull charge, the valve compensates and superheat barely moves — it can't tell you whether the charge is right.

What does respond to charge is the liquid stacking in the condenser — i.e. subcooling. Add refrigerant → subcooling climbs; recover → it drops. That's a clean, direct charging signal.

How you charge: weigh/adjust to the subcooling target on the data plate (commonly 8–12 °F). Superheat is just a check that the valve is doing its job.

Fixed orifice / piston

Charge to superheat

A piston is a fixed restriction — it can't modulate. So superheat is free to float with charge and load: add refrigerant → more liquid fills the coil → superheat falls; pull charge → the coil starves → superheat rises. That makes superheat the meaningful indicator.

There's no single target number. You read the manufacturer's charging chart, which sets the target superheat from the outdoor dry-bulb and indoor wet-bulb on the day.

How you charge: adjust to the chart's target superheat. On a fixed orifice, subcooling is informational only.

Read the symptoms

Faults & what the data shows

Superheat and subcooling together put you in one of the four corners, but the full picture needs the gauge pressures, the splits, and airflow. Here's what each common fault does to the readings — and the trap that catches techs most: a liquid-line restriction that wears an undercharge's disguise.

The one techs misread

Liquid-line restriction

Something is choking the high-pressure liquid on its way to the metering device — a clogged filter-drier, a kinked or crushed liquid line, debris or a screen, or a stuck/partly-closed metering device. Mass flow drops, so the evaporator, compressor, and condenser all run starved.

Liquid stacks up before the restriction (so condenser subcooling stays normal-to-high), while the evaporator starves after it (so superheat climbs and suction pressure falls). High superheat AND high/normal subcooling at the same time is the restriction signature.

Don't mistake it for an undercharge. Both show high superheat and low suction — so a tech reaching for a hose can dump refrigerant into a system that isn't low, and end up overcharged on top of a restriction. Subcooling is the tiebreaker: an undercharge runs LOW subcooling (little liquid in the condenser); a restriction holds NORMAL-to-HIGH subcooling (liquid stacked behind the choke point).

Confirm it at the part: a real restriction shows a temperature drop across the drier or fitting — it'll sweat or even frost right at the choke point while the line downstream is warmer/cooler than it should be. Low compressor amp draw backs it up. Find and clear the restriction; don't add charge.

Symptom guide — the common faults

Superheat / subcooling ranges are the same bands the Scenarios and Practice diagnosis use. Directions are relative to a healthy system — actual psig depends on the refrigerant and the day's conditions, which the bench computes live. Always confirm airflow (indoor and outdoor) and the splits, and defer to the data plate before condemning a charge.

Refrigerant-quality problems

Noncondensables (air / moisture). Air or nitrogen that won't condense collects in the top of the condenser and pushes head pressure up — higher than the outdoor temperature should produce. Subcooling can read normal-to-high but capacity is poor and the high side runs hot. Tell: the high-side saturation temperature sits well above outdoor + a normal split and won't square with a clean PT relationship. Cause: weak evacuation, a low-side leak drawing air in, or moisture. Fix: recover, evacuate to a deep vacuum (micron gauge), replace the drier.

Moisture freezing at the metering device. Water in the system freezes at the coldest restriction — the orifice or valve — and intermittently blocks flow. The unit cools fine, then shows restriction symptoms (high superheat, low suction, starved coil) until the ice melts and it recovers. A classic drier/moisture call.

Wrong or mixed refrigerant. If the gauge's saturation temperature won't line up with the measured line temperatures on a system you believe is correct, suspect the charge itself — air, the wrong refrigerant, or a blend that's lost composition. The PT chart only holds for the pure, correct refrigerant. None of R-22 / R-410A / R-32 / R-454B are drop-in interchangeable.

Metering-device faults

TXV stuck closed / underfeeding. Looks just like a restriction — high evaporator superheat, low suction, starved coil — but the choke point is the valve itself. Check the sensing bulb charge and mounting before condemning the valve.

TXV stuck open / overfeeding or hunting. The valve isn't controlling, so superheat falls toward zero (low superheat, floodback risk), often with high subcooling. Superheat that swings up and down is a hunting valve.

Fixed orifice / piston. No valve to fail, but the piston or its inlet screen can clog (a restriction), and with no modulation the superheat swings with charge and load. That's exactly why you charge a fixed orifice to superheat off the chart, and a TXV to subcooling.

A2L note. R-32 and R-454B are mildly flammable — A2L-rated tools, recovery, and leak detection, and EPA 608 to handle any of it.

Charging method by metering device

TXV / EEV: charge to subcooling (target per the data plate, commonly 8–12 °F). The valve modulates to hold superheat, so superheat is a check, not the charging target — typically landing 8–15 °F at the evaporator.

Fixed orifice / piston: charge to superheat using the manufacturer's charging chart, which sets a target superheat from outdoor dry-bulb and indoor wet-bulb. There is no single fixed number, and subcooling is informational only.

Evaporator superheat vs total (compressor) superheat: measured back at the condensing unit, suction-line heat gain adds on top, so total superheat often reads higher (frequently 20 °F+). Always compare against where the manufacturer specifies the measurement.

Why dew vs bubble for a blend

A zeotropic blend changes temperature as it changes state at constant pressure — the glide. In the evaporator the last liquid finishes boiling at the dew point, so vapor superheat is measured above dew. In the condenser the last vapor finishes condensing at the bubble point, so liquid subcooling is measured below bubble.

R-22 and R-32 are pure — one number. R-410A is near-azeotropic, glide under ~0.3 °F, so one column is fine. R-454B glides ~1.5 °F; grabbing the wrong column throws the answer off by about that much.

A2L safety. R-32 and R-454B are mildly flammable (A2L). They need A2L-rated gauges, recovery equipment, and leak detection — R-410A tooling is not automatically rated. None of these four are drop-in interchangeable, and EPA Section 608 certification is required to handle refrigerant.
Data sources & honest limits

R-22 & R-410A — Arkema Forane PT charts, generated from the NIST REFPROP database (saturation pressure, °F, psig; R-410A shown as liquid/vapor).
R-454B — Honeywell Solstice® 454B PT chart (bubble / dew, psig), cross-checked against the A-Gas A2L PT chart.
R-32 — A-Gas A2L PT chart, cross-checked against an independent published table (one outlier point at 80 °F corrected to fit the curve).
Composition, glide, GWP & safety class — Honeywell, Chemours/Opteon and A-Gas technical literature.
